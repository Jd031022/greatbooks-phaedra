Literary Analysis

Examining Racine's Masterpiece

Structure & Form

Racine's Phaedra exemplifies French classical tragedy, adhering strictly to the rules established by 17th-century French dramaturgy. The play consists of five acts, maintains the three unities, and employs alexandrine verse (twelve-syllable lines with a caesura).

Unity of Time

The entire action takes place within 24 hours, creating intense dramatic pressure.

Unity of Place

All events occur in the royal palace at Troezen, emphasizing the claustrophobic atmosphere.

Unity of Action

A single plotline focuses exclusively on Phaedra's forbidden passion and its consequences.

Language & Verse

Racine's alexandrine verse is renowned for its elegance, rhythmic precision, and emotional intensity. The constrained form paradoxically allows for profound psychological exploration. Key speeches, such as Phaedra's confession to Oenone (Act I, Scene 3) and her declaration of love to Hippolytus (Act II, Scene 5), showcase Racine's mastery of rhetorical devices and emotional expression.

"My wound is older. I was struck by love
For Theseus' son, the youthful conqueror.
I saw him, and I blushed; I paled to see him;
A sudden turmoil troubled all my senses."

‚Äî Phaedra, Act I, Scene 3 (translated by R.C. Knight)

Psychological Depth

Racine's genius lies in his exploration of the human psyche. Phaedra is not simply a woman consumed by illicit desire but a complex character torn between passion and reason, moral conscience and overwhelming emotion.

Phaedra's Internal Conflict

Passion
Reason

Throughout the play, Phaedra struggles with guilt, shame, and self-loathing, making her one of literature's most psychologically complex tragic heroines.

Tragic Structure

The play follows Aristotle's principles of tragedy, featuring:

  • Hamartia: Phaedra's tragic flaw is her uncontrollable passion
  • Peripeteia: The reversal when Theseus returns, alive
  • Anagnorisis: Phaedra's recognition of her guilt before suicide
  • Catharsis: The audience's purgation of pity and fear

Themes & Motifs

Philosophical Underpinnings of the Tragedy

Forbidden Passion

The destructive power of illicit desire, particularly Phaedra's incestuous love for her stepson, which violates social and moral codes.

"Venus I feel in all my fever'd frame" ‚Äî Phaedra

Reason vs. Passion

The Jansenist conflict between human will and divine grace, reason and emotion, which Racine explores through his characters' struggles.

"I have deserved it all" ‚Äî Phaedra

Divine Intervention

The role of gods (Venus, Neptune) in determining human fate, reflecting both Greek mythology and Jansenist ideas of predestination.

"Neptune has sworn, and he will keep his oath" ‚Äî Theseus

Appearance vs. Reality

The contrast between public reputation and private truth, exemplified by Phaedra's false accusation against Hippolytus.

"My hands, trained not to punish innocence" ‚Äî Hippolytus

Death & Fate

The inevitability of tragic destiny, inherited guilt (Phaedra's family curse), and the inescapable nature of divine punishment.

"The die is cast, and I must follow fate" ‚Äî Phaedra

Royal Power & Duty

The burdens of monarchy, family obligations, and the conflict between personal desire and public responsibility.

"A king's commands are justified by all" ‚Äî Theseus

Philosophical Context

Racine's Phaedra reflects the Jansenist theology that influenced the playwright during his education at Port-Royal. Jansenism emphasized original sin, human depravity, and the necessity of divine grace for salvation‚Äîthemes mirrored in Phaedra's helplessness against her passion.

Jansenist Influence

The tragedy illustrates the Jansenist belief that humans are powerless without God's grace, as Phaedra struggles helplessly against her sinful desires.

Neoclassical Aesthetics

Racine adheres to the French classical ideal of "plaire et instruire" (to please and instruct), balancing emotional intensity with moral instruction.

Greek Heritage

While adapting Euripides, Racine adds psychological depth and moral complexity, transforming the ancient myth for 17th-century audiences.
